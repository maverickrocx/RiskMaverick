Enterprise Risk Register — Top-Down & Bottom-Up

An effective risk register is built from both directions simultaneously — Board-level strategic risks flowing down, and granular loss events and RCSA outputs flowing up. The two streams meet at the business unit level.

Top-Down Stream (Board → Desk)
Board sets Risk Appetite Statement → CRO translates to risk limits → Desks operate within those limits. Strategic risks (geopolitical, regulatory change, ESG) are identified at Group level and cascaded.
Bottom-Up Stream (Events → Escalation)
Loss events, near-misses, and KRI breaches reported by business units → RCSA assessment → Risk Committee escalation → Risk Register updated. Drives RCSA heatmap refresh and LDA recalibration.
RCSA Heatmap: Inherent vs Residual Risk
Every risk is rated on a 5×5 Likelihood × Impact grid twice: (1) Inherent Risk — before any controls; (2) Residual Risk — after controls. The gap between them measures control effectiveness. Red residual risks require Board-level action plans.

Coherent Risk Measures — Why ES Replaced VaR

VaR has a critical flaw: it can show a higher number for a diversified portfolio than the sum of its parts — penalising diversification. Expected Shortfall (ES) fixes this by averaging all losses beyond the VaR threshold, not just counting whether the threshold was breached. Basel FRTB now mandates ES at 97.5% confidence over VaR at 99%.

Coherence PropertyVaRES (CVaR)
Sub-additive (diversification reward)FailsPasses
Monotone (more loss = more risk)PassesPasses

Extreme Value Theory (EVT) — Modelling the Far Tail

When energy shocks or financial crises dominate risk, EVT provides a statistically rigorous framework for the far tail beyond observed data — where standard normal-distribution models dramatically underestimate.

Block Maxima Approach
Takes the single worst loss in each time block (e.g. worst week in each month). Fits a heavy-tailed distribution to those maximums. Best for annual capital tail estimates where you care about the single catastrophic event per period.
Peaks Over Threshold Approach
Uses every loss above a high threshold — not just the single worst. More data-efficient and the preferred approach in practice. The risk manager sets the threshold; the quant fits the tail distribution above it.

Backtesting Framework — Basel Traffic Light System

Daily VaR predictions are compared against actual P&L. Exception counts over the trailing 250-day window determine the zone, which drives the capital multiplier applied to the VaR-based capital charge.

ZoneExceptions / 250 daysCapital MultiplierAction
Green0 – 43.00×No action required
Amber5 – 93.40 – 3.85×Investigate model quality
Red10+4.00× (maximum)Mandatory model review
Statistical Test for Model Validity
The exception count over 250 days is tested to check whether it's statistically consistent with the model's confidence level. Two key errors matter: Type I — declaring a good model broken (wastes capital). Type II — accepting a bad model that understates risk (the more dangerous regulatory failure).

Estimating Probability of Default (PD) — How Risk Managers Do It

PD can be estimated from three sources: internal ratings (historical default rates by grade), market signals (CDS spreads, equity-implied distance-to-default), and structural models that infer asset health from observable equity data. Risk managers typically triangulate all three before setting a credit limit.

CVA / DVA — What Risk Managers Track

Credit Valuation Adjustment (CVA) is the mark-to-market cost of counterparty default risk embedded in a derivative — what you would charge (or discount) to account for the chance the counterparty doesn't pay. DVA is the mirror: own-credit adjustment from the counterparty's perspective. Both appear on the P&L and in the daily risk report.

MetricWhat It MeasuresWhere It Appears
Expected Exposure (EE)Average amount we're owed at a future date across many price scenarios — the "most likely" exposureCVA calculation, daily CCR report
Potential Future Exposure (PFE)95th percentile of what we could be owed — the conservative credit limit numberCounterparty credit limits, ISDAs
Expected Positive Exposure (EPE)Time-averaged EE across the full trade life — the key input to regulatory CCR capitalBasel SA-CCR capital charge
Net CVACVA minus DVA — the net P&L adjustment for bilateral counterparty risk on the derivatives bookDaily P&L, IFRS 13 fair value
Wrong-Way Risk (WWR): When counterparty PD is positively correlated with our exposure — e.g. buying CDS protection from a bank heavily exposed to the same reference entity. EE and PD spike together, causing standard CVA models to dramatically underestimate the true adjustment.

Loss Distribution Approach (LDA) — Op Risk Capital Modelling

LDA separately models loss frequency and severity for each Basel business-line × event-type cell, then combines them via Monte Carlo convolution to produce an aggregate annual loss distribution. Capital = 99.9th percentile − Expected Loss.

Step 1 — Frequency Model
How often do loss events occur? Fitted to the annual count of internal loss events per business line and event type. Clustering behaviour (e.g. trading errors spiking during volatile markets) requires a heavier-tailed frequency assumption.
Step 2 — Severity Model
How large is each individual loss? A right-skewed distribution fits most operational losses (many small, a few enormous). The far tail — covering catastrophic events like rogue trading — is calibrated using ORX external consortium loss data where internal history is insufficient.
Step 3 — Monte Carlo Simulation (10,000+ runs)
Randomly draw a number of events (from Step 1), then sum that many individual loss draws (from Step 2). Repeat 10,000 times to build the full annual aggregate loss distribution. Op Risk Capital = 99.9th percentile loss minus expected loss.

Four T's of Risk Treatment

Every RCSA-identified risk must be assigned a treatment strategy. The choice depends on likelihood × impact vs. cost of control. No risk should sit unassigned in the register.

Tolerate
Accept the risk. Within appetite and cost of control exceeds benefit. Monitor via KRI with agreed threshold.
e.g. Low-frequency minor settlement fails within budget tolerance
Treat
Implement controls to reduce likelihood or impact. Most common response for amber RCSA risks.
e.g. Add dual-authorisation on trades >$1M
Transfer
Shift risk to a third party via insurance, outsourcing, or contractual indemnity. Risk cost moves off-balance-sheet.
e.g. Cyber insurance, D&O liability cover
Terminate
Exit the activity. Risk exceeds appetite and cannot be controlled cost-effectively. Board-level decision required.
e.g. Withdraw from a sanctioned market or product

Operational Resilience moves beyond preventing losses to ensuring critical business services can withstand, adapt to, and recover from severe but plausible disruptions — even when individual controls fail. Mandated by FCA/PRA (UK), DORA (EU), and OCC (US) from 2022–2025.

Liquidity-Adjusted VaR (LVaR)

Standard VaR assumes you can exit a position instantly at mid-market. That's rarely true for large or illiquid books. LVaR adjusts VaR upward to account for two real-world costs: the time it takes to unwind (scaling VaR by the square root of exit days) and the bid-ask spread paid on the way out.

What Drives LVaR HigherWhy It Matters
Longer exit horizonA 10-day unwind window produces roughly 3× the 1-day VaR — market can move further against you
Wide bid-ask spreadIlliquid assets (structured credit, physical commodities) carry large spread cost on top of price risk
Large position relative to market depthSelling a position that is 10% of daily market volume will move the price against you — market impact adds to VaR
Example: A $100M illiquid bond with a 50bps bid-ask spread and 5-day exit horizon adds $0.25M in liquidation cost on top of 1-day VaR — easily the dominant risk for low-volatility assets held in size.

Treasury Portfolio Strategies

Treasury manages the HQLA buffer and ALM book using three core duration strategies — chosen based on the interest rate outlook and the firm's specific liquidity horizon requirements.

Ladder Strategy
Equal allocation across all maturities. Maturing assets automatically reinvested at current rates. Simultaneously reduces reinvestment risk and liquidity risk. Lowest management overhead. Optimal in stable rate environments.
Barbell Strategy
Concentrated at very short (liquidity) and very long (yield) maturities — nothing in the middle. High convexity benefits, better yield than ladder. Requires active rebalancing as yield curve shifts.
Carry Strategy
Borrow short, invest long — earning the yield spread (positive carry). Maximises net interest income but creates funding gap. Requires a robust Contingency Funding Plan (CFP). Risky in inverted yield curves (2022–2023).
